01

Concepts

The core ideas the report is organized around. CTEM is the through-line; the rest qualify it.

  • CTEM Continuous Threat Exposure Management

    Gartner-coined framework for continuously identifying, prioritizing, validating, and remediating exposures across an organization's attack surface. Treats security as an always-on cycle (scope → discover → prioritize → validate → mobilize) rather than a periodic audit.

    The whole report is organized around CTEM-maturity self-ratings. Almost every chart cuts the data by CTEM maturity.

  • Exposure management EM

    Broader umbrella for the practice of finding, evaluating, and reducing security exposures (misconfigurations, unpatched software, identity weaknesses, accessible assets, etc.). CTEM is the continuous flavor; "exposure management" without qualifier may include periodic programs.

  • Validation of exploitability

    Confirming that a discovered vulnerability or exposure is actually exploitable in the organization's specific environment — i.e. that an attacker could realistically reach it and use it. The opposite of treating every CVE as equally urgent.

    Figure 2 is the validation-failure story: 97% of orgs say they have difficulty validating exploitability, with the cohort-gap concentrating on the skills/expertise barrier (+12pp for Laggards).

    Source: Q17combi3

  • Consolidated view of risk

    A unified, real-time picture of the organization's risk posture across all assets, tools, and business units — the opposite of a fragmented stack where each tool surfaces its own findings.

    Used as a cohort dimension in Figures 2 and 5: respondents are split into three buckets (Fully consolidated / Mostly / Partial or none) from the ExposureManagement screener.

  • PIR Priority Intelligence Requirements

    A threat-intelligence methodology where the organization defines the specific threats and questions it most needs answered ("which APT groups target our sector? what TTPs do they use?"), then drives intel collection toward those questions instead of consuming generic feeds.

    Shows up in Bonus 6 pair 2: 95% say they'd benefit from establishing PIRs, but only 38% use threat intel in a continuous, fully automated validation process.

    Source: Q24_1 · Q15

  • Threat intelligence TI

    External information about attacker activity — indicators of compromise (IOCs), tactics/techniques/procedures (TTPs), campaigns, infrastructure. Consumed via commercial feeds, open sources, ISACs, or internal collection. Effective TI is contextual and actionable; the report's tension is that most respondents say their TI isn't yet effective at prioritization.

  • MTT-D / MTT-R / MTT-Remediate Mean Time To Detect / Respond / Remediate

    Three sequential stages of the exposure window: how long it takes to detect a new exposure, respond to it (triage, investigate), and remediate it (patch, mitigate, accept). Each was asked as a 5-bucket distribution (< 1 day, 1–2 days, 2–5 days, > 5 days, not measured).

    Bonus 7 visualizes these. Means: Detect 1.77 days · Respond 1.88 · Remediate 2.35.

    Source: Q18_1 · Q18_2 · Q18_3

  • Dead-end investigation

    Time a security analyst spends investigating a risk that turns out to be low-priority or non-exploitable. The report measures this as a percentage of total analyst time (Q13 mean). The visualization translates 42% of a 40-hour week ≈ 16.8 hours/analyst/week into a unit chart.

    Source: Q13 (mean, n=550)

02

Cohorts & bases

Every chart that shows a "cohort split" is using one of these segments. The base sizes matter for confidence.

  • Mature CTEM

    Respondents who self-described as having "CTEM in place and continuously refining" on the ExposureManagement screener. The "leaders" cohort across most cohort cuts.

    Base size: n=252

  • Planning CTEM

    Respondents who self-described as "planning to adopt CTEM" — they haven't started yet but have it on the roadmap. The smallest CTEM-maturity tier and the "laggard" cohort for figures 1, 3, and 4.

    Base size: n=40  ·  small base — read cohort comparisons as directional, not statistically tight.

  • Fully consolidated view

    Respondents who reported a fully consolidated view of risk across the organization. The "leaders" cohort for figures 2 and 5.

    Base size: n=224

  • Mostly consolidated view

    The middle tier — partial consolidation in place but with gaps. Used as the mid-cohort in figures 2 and 5.

    Base size: n=269

  • Partial / limited / no view

    The "laggards" cohort in figures 2 and 5 — respondents whose view of risk is fragmented, limited, or absent.

    Base size: n=57  ·  smaller base; cohort gaps in this group have wider uncertainty.

  • Regional splits

    The report doesn't visualize regional splits, but they're available in every sheet's cross-tab columns.

03

Tool categories

The nine tool acronyms in Figure 1 (Q6). All are common in cybersecurity stacks; the split between continuous and periodic is the editorial frame used in the charts.

  • CSPM continuous
    Cloud Security Posture Management — continuous scanning of cloud-account configurations against best-practice and compliance benchmarks. Adoption: 57% total · 59% Mature CTEM · 38% Planning.
  • EASM continuous
    External Attack Surface Management — discovers and monitors the assets exposed to the public internet from the outside-in. Adoption: 47%.
  • IAM / PAM continuous
    Identity / Privileged Access Management — controls who can authenticate to what, with extra scrutiny on accounts with elevated privileges. Adoption: 47%.
  • GRC continuous
    Governance, Risk & Compliance — platform layer that ties controls to frameworks (NIST, ISO 27001, SOC 2). Adoption: 45%, but with a notable cohort split (50% Mature vs 28% Planning).
  • BAS continuous
    Breach & Attack Simulation — automated, continuous adversary emulation that runs known attack chains against the live environment to test defenses. Adoption: 44%.
  • TI feeds periodic
    Threat Intelligence feeds — external streams of indicators and reporting. Coded as periodic in the chart because most orgs consume them in batch rather than continuously operationalize them. Adoption: 45%.
  • Vuln scanning periodic
    Vulnerability scanning — typically scheduled scans (weekly, monthly) of internal assets against CVE databases. Adoption: 42%.
  • Pen testing periodic
    Penetration testing — manual, time-bound red-team engagements. The most-periodic of the nine, and the one Mature-CTEM orgs are most willing to drop in favor of continuous tooling. Adoption: 41%.
  • Custom scripts periodic
    In-house tooling — bespoke detection or assessment scripts. Adoption: 25%.

04

Survey methodology

How Vanson Bourne ran the field and how to read the numbers.

  • Field period & project

    Vanson Bourne, project 2025112501DG, fielded Feb–Mar 2026. Online quantitative survey of 550 security decision-makers across North America (200), EMEA (200), and APAC (150).

  • Multi-select

    Respondents can pick more than one answer. Total of all answer %s usually exceeds 100%. Used in Q6 (tools), Q20 (AI areas), Q23 (drivers), etc.

  • Combined rank 1/2/3 combi3

    The respondent was asked to rank a list of options. The combi3 sheet sums the % of respondents who placed an option in their top three — so each respondent contributes to multiple rows. Used heavily in Q10 (buying criteria) and Q17 (validation barriers).

  • Rank-1

    Same source data as combi3, but only the single highest-ranked option per respondent is counted. Useful when you want to know the most-important factor rather than the broad top-3.

  • Mean / median

    For numeric questions (Q13 % time, Q19 % AI-driven, Q18 days), the bottom rows of the sheet give the cohort mean and median. The whitepaper leads with means; the medians are usually close.

  • Don't knows / Not applicable

    For Q19 and a few others, "don't know" responses are excluded and percentages are renormalized to the remaining base. The difference between renormalized and raw is sub-percent across all questions checked.

05

Spreadsheet conventions

How the Vanson Bourne workbook is structured. Naming patterns repeat across all 80+ tabs.

  • QN
    A single-question sheet (e.g. Q6, Q23). One sheet, all the splits as columns.
  • QN_M
    Multi-statement questions — each statement gets its own sheet. Q14 has six (Q14_1 … Q14_6), each measuring agreement with one statement.
  • QN Summary
    Consolidated view for a multi-statement question — usually mean and top-2-box agreement scores per statement.
  • QNcombi3
    "Combined ranked 1st, 2nd, or 3rd" view for a ranking question. The dataset behind almost all the multi-barrier / multi-driver charts.
  • QNrank1
    "Top-rank only" view for the same question — counts only the respondent's #1 choice.
  • Profile / Screener sheets

    Demographic and qualification questions: HiddenProfileOrganisationSector, ProfileOrganisationSize, ProfileDepartment, SecurityResponsibility, ExposureManagement, ThreatIntelligence, ProfilePosition.

    ExposureManagement is the source of the CTEM-maturity cohorts (Mature / Mostly / Planning) and the consolidated-view cohorts (Fully / Mostly / Partial) — most cohort splits in the charts trace back here.

  • Sheet anatomy

    Each question sheet is a wide-format cross-tab: answer options run down the rows, then paired Count | % columns repeat for every demographic split (Total → Region → Country → Sector → Org Size → Department → Role).

    Row 5–7 contain the split-group headers · row 8 has the base size for each split · data rows follow · bottom rows are Total / Mean / median where applicable.

    Percentages are column %, not row %. Don't recompute from counts unless you need a different base.

06

Units & notation

Small but constant — these symbols appear on almost every chart.

  • %
    A column percentage within a specific cohort base (or the total). Never a row %, never a count.
  • pp percentage points

    The absolute difference between two percentages. "+12pp" means the second value is 12 percentage points higher than the first. Not the same as "12% more" (which would be a relative change).

    Example: a barrier cited by 46% of Leaders vs 58% of Laggards = a +12pp gap, not a 26% gap.

  • n=
    Base size — number of respondents in this cohort. Where you see n=550, the chart uses the full sample. Where you see n=40 (Planning CTEM), read cohort comparisons with that base in mind.
  • Δ / "gap"
    A cohort gap — usually Laggards − Leaders or Mature − Planning, signed. Positive = the metric hurts laggards more (or matters less to mature orgs); negative = the opposite. Always given in pp.
  • Top-2-box
    For agree/disagree-style scales, the sum of "Strongly agree" + "Agree". Used in Q4/Q14/Q22/Q24 summary sheets.
